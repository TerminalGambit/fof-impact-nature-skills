# IMP Five Dimensions — Full Reference

The Impact Management Project (IMP) framework defines five dimensions of impact that any investment thesis or impact report should address. Now stewarded by the Impact Frontiers initiative.

## 1. WHAT

Tells us what outcome occurs in the period, how important the outcome is to stakeholders, and whether it is positive or negative.

**Data categories:**
- Outcome in period
- Outcome threshold (level at which the outcome is meaningful to stakeholders)
- Importance of outcome to stakeholder
- SDG / global goal alignment

## 2. WHO

Tells us which stakeholders experience the outcome and how underserved they are.

**Data categories:**
- Stakeholder type (consumer, worker, community, planet, etc.)
- Geographical boundary
- Outcome level at baseline (relative to threshold)
- Stakeholder characteristics (demographics, vulnerability)

## 3. HOW MUCH

Tells us how significant the outcome is in terms of three sub-dimensions: scale, depth, duration.

**Scale:** number of stakeholders experiencing the outcome
**Depth:** degree of change experienced by stakeholders (often measured as % movement from baseline toward threshold)
**Duration:** time period stakeholders experience the outcome

## 4. CONTRIBUTION

Tells us whether an enterprise's and/or investor's efforts resulted in outcomes that were likely better than what would have occurred otherwise.

**Enterprise contribution:**
- Depth counterfactual: would stakeholders have experienced this depth without the enterprise?
- Duration counterfactual: would the outcome have lasted as long?
- Scale counterfactual: would as many stakeholders have been reached?

**Investor contribution (one or more of):**
- Signal that impact matters (capital flow signal)
- Engage actively (governance, expertise, networks)
- Grow new/undersupplied capital markets (e.g., first institutional capital into a category)
- Provide flexible capital (patient, concessional, blended)

## 5. RISK

Tells us the likelihood that the impact will be different than expected.

**Nine risk types:**
1. **Evidence risk** — insufficient high-quality data to know what impact is occurring
2. **External risk** — external factors disrupt ability to deliver impact
3. **Stakeholder participation risk** — stakeholders don't experience outcome as expected because they don't engage as expected
4. **Drop-off risk** — positive impact does not endure / negative impact recurs
5. **Efficiency risk** — impact could have been achieved at lower cost or fewer negative side effects
6. **Execution risk** — activities not delivered as planned
7. **Alignment risk** — impact is not locked into enterprise model and incentives may drift
8. **Endurance risk** — required activities are not delivered for long enough to yield impact
9. **Unexpected impact risk** — significant unexpected positive or negative impact materializes

## A/B/C investment classification (per IMP)

Investments are categorized by what they contribute:

- **A — Act to avoid harm:** Prevent or reduce significant ESG risks
- **B — Benefit stakeholders:** Generate positive effects for people or planet (in addition to acting to avoid harm)
- **C — Contribute to solutions:** Generate substantial positive change for underserved or planetary stakeholders (in addition to acting to avoid harm)

Most "impact" investments aim for C. Most ESG investments target A. Many "sustainable" investments are B but called C.
