# Vintage Diversification & Commitment Pacing

## Why this matters

In private markets, vintage year is destiny. The best venture fund of 2009 outperformed the best of 2007 by orders of magnitude not because of GP skill but because of macro positioning. A FoF that piles all commitments into 2021-2022 will live with that vintage's outcomes regardless of GP quality.

Vintage diversification is the single most important portfolio construction lever for an FoF — more important than GP selection at the margin.

## The vintage layering framework

Target: roughly even capital deployment across 3-5 consecutive vintages, with annual commitment dollars adjusted to total NAV growth and pacing model.

### Steady-state commitment pacing formula

For a steady-state portfolio:
```
Annual commitment $ ≈ Target portfolio NAV × (1 / Average fund life in years) × Recycling factor
```

Where:
- Average fund life: typically 10-12 years for PE, 8-10 for VC, 12-15 for infra/real assets
- Recycling factor: 1.0 if no distribution reinvestment; lower if distributions are spent

### Adjustments for portfolio phase

**Building phase (0-5 years):**
- Over-commit by 20-40% vs steady-state formula
- Heavy 1st-tier manager concentration acceptable to establish relationships
- Vintage layering takes 4-5 years to develop

**Steady-state phase (5-10 years):**
- Stick to pacing formula
- Re-up decisions are most important commitment lever
- Single-manager exposure should respect concentration policy

**Mature phase (10+ years):**
- Commitment dollars sized to NAV growth + distribution recycling
- Manager turnover becomes intentional portfolio management lever
- Secondary sales become tool for re-balancing

## The J-curve management problem

In a single fund, the J-curve (negative early TVPI from fees + early write-downs, before exits start distributing) lasts 4-6 years.

In a FoF portfolio with proper vintage layering, the aggregate J-curve compresses dramatically:
- Year 1: pure J-curve drag (negative TVPI)
- Year 3: first commitments approaching mid-life, some distributions starting
- Year 5: mature portfolio with steady distributions offsetting new commitments
- Year 7+: steady-state distributions ≥ new commitments

To compress the J-curve further:
- **Mix in secondaries** at portfolio inception — secondaries have shorter J-curves
- **Mix in late-stage / growth funds** which deploy faster than early-stage venture
- **Include co-invests** which have minimal J-curve (no fund-level fees)
- **Avoid clustering all commitments in one fund manager** which concentrates J-curve timing

## Vintage concentration risk

Heuristic: no more than 25-30% of total commitments in any single vintage year.

When this happens unintentionally, common causes:
- Many GPs fundraise simultaneously (post-correction bunching)
- LP under-deployed in prior years and "catches up"
- Single sector/theme heat causes many specialist GPs to fundraise at once
- LP allocator changed and the new team commits aggressively

Remediation:
- Pause new commitments to thin overweighted vintages
- Selectively pursue secondary purchases of off-vintage interests
- Consider tactical vehicle (e.g., later-vintage fund-of-one) to fill gaps

## Vintage diversification + impact mandates

Impact and natural capital funds add a wrinkle:
- **Longer deployment periods** — nature-based asset funds may have 5-7 year investment periods (vs 3-5 for standard PE)
- **Longer time-to-impact** — regeneration, biodiversity recovery, and carbon sequestration may take 5-15 years to measure
- **Vintage benchmark scarcity** — impact / natural capital vintages don't yet have Cambridge / Burgiss-level benchmark depth

Implications:
- Pacing model should target slightly longer fund lives (12-15 years average)
- Vintage layering may need 6-8 vintages rather than 4-5 for diversification
- Benchmark constraints mean GP selection has even higher weight than vintage diversification
- Consider evergreen / open-ended vehicles where vintage diversification is structural rather than commitment-timing
